What Makes a Keystone Species?

Understanding the disproportionate impact certain species have on their ecosystems

The Keystone Concept

1969
"A species whose effect is large, and disproportionately large relative to its abundance." - Robert T. Paine

Just as a keystone in an arch supports the entire structure, keystone species support entire ecosystems through their activities, despite often having small populations.

The Arch Analogy

Other Species
Keystone Species
Other Species

Remove the keystone, and the entire arch collapses. Remove a keystone species, and the ecosystem collapses.

Disproportionate Impact

Their ecological impact is much larger than their abundance would suggest

80% Impact vs. Abundance

Critical Function

Perform essential ecosystem services that no other species can replace

Unique Role in Ecosystem

Cascade Effects

Their presence or absence creates ripple effects through multiple trophic levels

Ecosystem Health Indicator

Predators
